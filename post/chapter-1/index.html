QuasVar - Quasar Variability Study

August 30, 2020

Active galactic nuclei (AGN) are the extremely luminous, supermassive black holes (SMBH) at the core of some galaxies. Active galaxies and their nuclei are classified as being active because of their extreme radiative power; however, there are many galaxies that fit under this category and they seem to be extremely varied with few similarities beyond their unbelievable luminosity. Because this class of objects is so varied, astronomers have been attempting to build a model that explains their inner structure and differences for decades. The unified model of AGN (the most common current theory) postulates that all AGN are the same object, but viewed at a different angle which changes the way emitted light reaches the observer. The diagram below, borrowed from this paper shows how this angle of viewing produces different objects.

In any case, emitted light from AGN is stochastically variable - it moves up and down at random with no discernible period. Studying the relationship between properties of the variation in light and the properties of the black hole at the center of an AGN is important to better understanding how these extreme objects function. Further analysis could yield qualifications of the unified model of AGN.

Elizabeth Sellers and I are conducting research that hopes to shed more light on a specific type of AGN known as quasars. The type of data fundamental to this research is known as a lightcurve, which is a dataset containing measurements of the brightness/flux from an object over time. We use data from the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) to gather these lightcurves. This is slightly unprecedented, as TESS lightcurves are usually used to identify and study exoplanets. However, TESS is useful to us because of its long-term, short timescale observations. In order to conduct our study, there were essentially three steps:

  1. Create a catalogue of quasars with known properties to study.
  2. Gather their lightcurves.
  3. Analyze the data in a useful way that brings about meaningful conclusions.

In this post we will walk through these steps in detail.

Creating the Quasar Catalogue

This step seems the easiest; how hard can it possibly be to find a list of quasars with known properties (mass, luminosity, etc.)? Originally, before we even began looking at using quasars instead of all AGN, we used a catalogue of AGN candidates built by a random forest classifier acting on Gaia and unWISE data. This catalogue eventually proved to not be terribly useful to us, as these objects, while mostly AGN, could not be guaranteed as such. The catalogue also possessed no auxiliary property data, so even if we had been able to generate lightcurves, we could not have analyzed them effectively.

We eventually found a catalogue that was able to meet our needs. The Sloan Digital Sky Survey publishes a quasar catalogue (Data Release 12 is current) which accurately identifies a large group of quasars. Furthermore, researchers have calculated black hole mass estimates for this catalogue that are a line-for-line match to the quasar catalogue, making integration easy.

Unfortunately, not all of the objects were suitable for use in this study. TESS has not observed all of the sky yet, and many of the objects are far too dim for TESS to accurately measure. TESS observes the sky in sectors: the satellite watches a single strip of the sky for a month, gathering data and reporting back to Earth, before moving on to the next sector. These sectors overlap at the ecliptic poles and exclude the ecliptic equator (to avoid catching solar system material). This is shown in the diagram below from the TESS Observatory Guide.

To eliminate these problems, we removed objects that had not been observed in any of the TESS sectors and were higher in apparent magnitude in the SDSS g-band than magnitude 20. In the future, we’ll likely re-make the catalogue using a much lower magnitude in the i-iand to make this cut, as the i-band is in near-infrared and is closer to the band TESS observes in. Additionally, TESS only aims for 50 ppm photometric precision on targets with a TESS magnitude 9-15. Creating the Lightcurves

In many ways, this was the hardest part of generating the dataset. On advice from our research advisor, we used a package known as eleanor to generate our lightcurves. However, this brought about a myriad of problems at first. For some reason, we couldn’t even install it on our computers. After months of work and getting in contact with the researcher who made this program (who was wonderful in helping us sort out the problem), we switched our primary research computer to Linux, which resolved the errors, as the program had not been built to function on Windows. Eventually, the eleanor dependency that made it incompatible with windows was removed and we were able to proceed.

The largest problem we faced in this area was the fact that quasars are certainly not what TESS was designed to observe. They are point sources that are dim and extremely far away. By virtue of these quasars all being part of an SDSS survey, there is SDSS imaging data available for all of them. Keep in mind that SDSS pixels are 0.396 arcseconds wide, while TESS pixels are 21 arcseconds wide, so something had to be 53 SDSS pixels wide to be 1 TESS pixel wide.

TESS publishes lightcurves for many targets that might have planets around them, but AGN are not among that list. To allow for other targets to be studied, TESS also publishes the images taken at a 30-minute cadence. These images are known as Full Frame Images (FFI) and must be cut down in order to generate lightcurves. To generate a lightcurve, one must find where the object of interest is on the FFI and then measure how the brightness of the object in that image changes over hundreds or thousands of images. Eleanor simplifies this process by doing most of it automatically and behind the scenes. In order to create good lightcurves, we contacted the only other researcher we could find using TESS on AGN, who recommended, among other things, using an extremely small aperture. In accordance with this advice, we created lightcurves using only on the single TESS pixel located over the quasar’s position. Eleanor automatically provides additional corrections, such as background subtraction. This outputs an array of brightness/flux values over time. We subtract the median flux to center the lightcurve on zero. Some example lightcurves are shown below, with outliers greater than 25 standard deviations from the mean clipped.

As you can see, these lightcurves look like a large amount of random noise. The question becomes, how much of this is due to the quasar emitting random noise and how much is instrument noise? We have very little way of measuring this, so we are currently focusing on improving our data integrity.

Modelling

We hope to use machine learning to model the relationship between the lightcurves and the properties of the quasars themselves. So far, we have tried many different algorithms and had no success. This is a very hard problem, admittedly, that could be compounded by unreliable data. This area is our current focus and will be updated as we move forward with this research.
